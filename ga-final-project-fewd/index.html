13 Illusions and Visual Tricks

At any given moment our visual cortex is processing colour, motion, orientation, and more. It’s a highly advanced system—but it isn’t perfect. It has certain biases. Enter cognitive illusions. Our vision makes assumption based on learning, memory and expectation, the result is that reality is not always what it seems.

Take a look at the examples below and see if you can spot the tricks.

Are the pink stripes different or the same?

This is known as the Munker-White Illusion. The pink stripes on the left will differ in perceived brightness from those on the right. The stripes on the left look darker but in fact, both sets of pink stripes are reflecting the same amount of light.

Hover over the image and watch carefully.

Why does this work? Our perception of an area's lightness is influenced by the surroundings to which the area appears to belong.

Can you see the grey blobs at the intersections of the grid?

This is known as the Hermann Grid. This works because of cells in our eyes called the retinal ganglion cells and their inhibition function. When you look at a part of the grid where no lines intersect, less light hits the inhibitory part of these cells. This results in the light pink area looking brighter.

If you look at the parts of the grid where the light pink lines intersect, there is more light pink relative to black, this means more inhibition occurs and you see a darker pink, or grey colour.

Hover over the image to see where the grey dots appear.

Is A or B the front of the cube?

The Necker cube is an optical illusion first published as a rhomboid in 1832 by Swiss crystallographer Louis Albert Necker.

It is a simple wire-frame drawing of a cube with no visual cues as to its orientation. In the case of this cube, you could interpret either the upper left (A) or the lower-right(B) square as its front side.

Hover over the image to reveal the front of the cube.

Which black line is longer?

The Ponzo illusion is a geometrical-optical illusion that was first demonstrated by the Italian psychologist Mario Ponzo (1882–1960) in 1911. He suggested that the human mind judges an object's size based on its background.

In this trick, a pair of converging lines distorts the perception of two identically sized lines. The line further to the right might look taller but it is in fact the same size as the line further to the left.

Hover over the image to prove the lines are the same size.

Is it small or just very far away?

This illusion is all about relative size perception. It was discovered by the German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus.

The central circle surrounded by large circles appears smaller than the central circle surrounded by small circles. Both the size of the surrounding circles and the distance they appear from the central circle, play a part in how we perceive the size of the central circles.

Hover over the image to prove the circles are the same size.

An impossible object

The Impossible Trident Figure was created by D. H Schuster, an American psychologist, who based it on an advertisement he saw in an aviation journal. It was first published in The American Journal of Psychology in 1964.

The left hand end of the figure appears to be a three pronged fork, but at the right end there appear to be only two.

Cognitive scientists have been interested in the processes involved in continuing to see impossible figures as possible even when we know them to be impossible. Why, for instance, do we not see the Impossible Trident just as some lines on a page once we realise that it can’t exist in three dimensional space?

The Golden Section

The golden section, or golden ration is a common mathematical ratio found in nature that can be used to create pleasing, natural looking compositions in design work.

The ration exists when a line is divided into two parts and the longer part (a) divided by the smaller part (b) is equal to the sum of (a) + (b) divided by (a), which both equal 1.618.

Maths aside, this ratio creates a sense of aesthetic beauty through harmony and proportion. The image shows the golden spiral, which pops up all over nature in the form of shells, plants and even hurricanes.

Deceptive Gradients

This is known as the Cornsweet Illusion. It was described in detail by Tom Cornsweet in the late 1960s.

The gradient on the left appears darker than the gradient on the right. In fact, the gradients are exactly the same.

Why? Because of something called lateral inhibition. In neurobiology, lateral inhibition is the capacity for an excited neuron to reduce the activity of its neighbours. The result is darker areas falsely appear even darker and lighter areas falsely appear even lighter.

An Undecidable Figure

This is an example of an impossible object (also known as an impossible figure or an undecidable figure). This series of cubes in perspective is based on the ‘Impossible Triangle’ often depicted by Swedish graphic artist Oscar Reutersvärd.

Follow this object all the way around with your eyes, it should play havoc with your brain's intuitive knowledge of physical laws. Even though we know this object cannot exist in three dimensional space, our brains still perceived it as three dimensional.

Overshooting in Typeface Design

Being mathematically precise doesn’t always provide visual or perceptual balance. The overshoot of a round or pointed letter (like O or A) is the degree to which it extends higher or lower than a comparably sized "flat" letter (like X or H), to achieve an optical effect of being the same size; it compensates for inaccuracies in human visual perception.

In this example the circle and the triangle have been scaled up slightly to compensate for the fact that they have less visual mass than the square.

This results in part of the circle and triangle protruding above the guideline. Not aligning them exactly with the square, makes for a more balanced composition.

The Triangle-Bisection Illusion

Icons can be deceiving, especially those with complex geometry and odd proportions. Not all icons within a set are symmetrical, pixel perfect or maintain a consistent aspect ratio. Some icons require direct intervention such as the play button.

Placing a triangle within a curved or straight container can make the element appear optically out of place. The reason for this is due to an effect known as the triangle-bisection illusion.

The solution is to nudge the triangle to the right, so the composition looks more balanced. Hover over the image to restore balance.

Are the long lines parallel?

The Zöllner Illusion was created by Johann Karl Friedrich Zöllner (1834 - 1882), a German astrophysicist with a keen interest in optical illusions. Zöllner was inspired by a cloth pattern that he observed in his father’s factory, and first published the illusion in the journal Annalen der Physik in 1860.

Examine the long lines with the short lines coming off them. Do they appear parallel to each other? The long lines parallelism appears to distort by other aspects of the image, in this case, the intersecting shorter lines.

Hover over the image to reveal if the lines are parallel.

Squares in motion

The squares in this optical illusion are actually square, though they appear distorted. The distortions are consistent with how squares in the real world would change their appearance if one were moving forward toward the center point.
