13 Optical Illusions and Visual Tricks.

At any given moment our visual cortex is processing colour, motion, orientation, and more. It’s a highly advanced system—but it isn’t perfect. It has certain biases. Enter optical illusions. Our vision makes assumption based on learning, memory and expectation, the result is reality is not always what it seems.

Have a good look at the examples below and see if you can spot the tricks.

Are the pink stripes different or the same?

This is known as the Munker-White Illusion. The pink stripes on the left will differ in perceived brightness from those on the rights. They look darker but in fact, both sets of stripes are reflecting the same amount of light.

Hover over the image and watch carefully.

Why does this work? Our perception of an area's lightness is influenced by the surroundings to which the area appears to belong.

Is A or B the front of the cube?

The Necker cube is an optical illusion first published as a rhomboid in 1832 by Swiss crystallographer Louis Albert Necker.

It is a simple wire-frame drawing of a cube with no visual cues as to its orientation. In the case of this cube, you could interpret either the to have either the upper right (A) or the lower-left(B) square as its front side.

Hover over the image to reveal the front of the cube.
